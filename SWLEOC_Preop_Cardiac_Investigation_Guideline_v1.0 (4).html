Key recommendations

R1  Do not request resting echocardiography or cardiac stress imaging routinely before elective arthroplasty. Investigate only to answer a defined clinical question whose result will change management.

R2  Assess functional capacity in every patient with ≥1 cardiac risk factor using the DASI (Appendix A). Do not rely on subjective MET estimation.

R3  Proceed without cardiac testing where the patient has no cardiovascular disease, risk factors or symptoms, OR good functional capacity (DASI >34 / ≥4 METs), OR a calculated MACE risk <1%.

R4  Request resting TTE only for a specific indication in Table 1.

R5  In the at-risk patient with poor or intermediate capacity, use NT-proBNP ± hs-troponin before considering imaging. NT-proBNP <300 ng/L with normal troponin → proceed with surveillance.

R6  Request stress imaging (DSE or SPECT-MPI) only when all four gates (Table 2) are satisfied.

R7  Do not pursue coronary angiography or revascularisation to “cover” surgery. Revascularise only for independent indications (ACS, >50% left main); prophylactic revascularisation does not improve perioperative outcomes.

R8  Defer elective arthroplasty after a recent coronary event or intervention per Table 3.

R9  Refer active cardiac conditions to cardiology / MDT and do not list until stabilised.

R10  Optimise anaemia, blood pressure, glycaemia and smoking, and arrange postoperative troponin surveillance where indicated.

1.  Purpose

This guideline sets the standard for preoperative cardiac investigation in adults undergoing elective hip and knee arthroplasty at SWLEOC. It answers two questions that drive disproportionate resource use, list cancellations and patient inconvenience: when is a resting echocardiogram justified, and when is cardiac stress imaging (dobutamine stress echocardiography or myocardial perfusion imaging) justified? It aligns SWLEOC practice with the 2024 AHA/ACC Multisociety guideline, the 2022 ESC guideline and NICE NG45, adapted to the elective-only setting in which deferral and optimisation are always available.

2.  Scope and applicability

  • In scope: all adults (≥18 years) listed for elective primary or revision hip or knee arthroplasty at SWLEOC.
  • Users: anaesthetists, pre-assessment staff and referring surgical teams.
  • Out of scope: emergency or trauma surgery and non-arthroplasty procedures (the principles may nonetheless inform practice). Patients with active cardiac conditions are managed via cardiology / MDT until stabilised, not through this pathway.

4.  The SWLEOC pathway

The algorithm below adapts the 2024 AHA/ACC logic (as distilled by Cohn, 2025) to elective arthroplasty. The emergency and time-sensitive branches are removed: at SWLEOC, deferral to optimise is always preferable to rushing to test. Tables 1–3 expand the decision points.

5.  Resting echocardiography — when it is (and is not) indicated

Table 1. Indications for resting transthoracic echocardiography before elective arthroplasty.

CategoryResting transthoracic echocardiography (TTE)
INDICATEDRequest before listing — a specific question must be answered:
• new murmur WITH cardiac symptoms (exertional dyspnoea, syncope, angina); • suspected severe/symptomatic valve disease (esp. aortic stenosis); • unexplained or worsening dyspnoea; • known HF/cardiomyopathy with a CHANGE in clinical status; • suspected pulmonary hypertension.
CONSIDERKnown valve disease or cardiomyopathy with NO study within ~12 months AND a change in status, or where the result would alter the anaesthetic plan or level of care. Order only if the answer changes management.
NOT INDICATEDDo NOT request for: routine pre-arthroplasty screening; asymptomatic patients with adequate functional capacity; hypertension, age, diabetes or a raised RCRI alone; “reassurance” or “completeness”; a stable patient with an adequate recent study. TTE does not predict perioperative ischaemic events and a normal study gives false reassurance.

How useful is it, really?

  • In a population cohort, echocardiography before major elective non-cardiac surgery was not associated with improved postoperative survival or shorter length of stay (Wijeysundera, BMJ 2011); the delay it introduces can cause harm.
  • The 2024 Appropriate Use Criteria rate resting and stress imaging as “rarely appropriate” in patients with no new/worsening symptoms and ≥4 METs — i.e. most arthroplasty patients with a reasonable history.
  • LVEF is a weak predictor of perioperative myocardial infarction and death. A normal study gives false reassurance; a mildly abnormal one rarely alters an intermediate-risk plan.
  • Where echo adds prognostic value at all, the increment is marginal and confined to a high-risk subset — not the general list.

6.  The case against routine investigation

  • The test changes nothing for most patients. At intermediate surgical risk with adequate capacity, post-test management is identical either way.
  • The “broken chain.” Even when disease is found, prophylactic coronary revascularisation before non-cardiac surgery does not improve outcomes (CARP; DECREASE-V). Revascularisation is reserved for independent indications — essentially ACS or >50% left-main stenosis.
  • Delay is not neutral. Each unnecessary test buys deconditioning, prolonged pain and immobility, opioid escalation, VTE exposure, cancelled lists and wasted theatre capacity — morbidity, for a quality-of-life operation.
  • The incidental-finding cascade. Diastolic dysfunction, trivial regurgitation and borderline measurements generate downstream tests, referrals and DAPT-related deferrals — each with its own risk and no benefit to the index operation.
  • Opportunity cost and equity. Echo and nuclear slots are finite; every unnecessary study displaces a patient with a genuine indication and lengthens their wait.
Guiding principle: request a cardiac test only for a patient who would warrant it independently of the surgery, and only when the result will change what we do.

7.  Cardiac stress imaging (DSE / SPECT-MPI) — the four gates

Stress imaging is a rule-out tool: strong negative predictive value (>90%), weak positive predictive value (0–37%). It reassures the genuinely uncertain patient; it is a poor instrument for “finding” disease to fix. Request DSE or SPECT-MPI only when ALL four gates are open.

Table 2. The four gates — all must be satisfied before requesting stress imaging.

GateRequirementWhy it matters
Gate 1
Functional capacity		Poor or genuinely unknown capacity: DASI <34 (≈<4 METs), not assessable by simpler means.Good capacity (DASI >34) predicts low risk; testing the fit patient changes nothing.
Gate 2
Surgical risk		Elevated MACE risk for THIS operation (e.g. complex revision, very high comorbidity).Primary arthroplasty is intermediate-risk; routine cases rarely clear this gate.
Gate 3
No active condition		No ACS, decompensated HF, severe/symptomatic AS, uncontrolled arrhythmia or severe PH.These are contraindications to stress testing — they go to cardiology, not a stress lab.
Gate 4
Will change management		A positive result would alter the decision to proceed, the level of care, or trigger an independently-indicated intervention.Prophylactic revascularisation does not improve perioperative outcomes (CARP); a positive test that changes nothing should not be done.

Choosing the modality

  • Exercise testing is preferred whenever the patient can exercise — but the arthritic joint is usually the limiter, which is what pushes these patients toward pharmacological/imaging stress.
  • Dobutamine stress echo (DSE): for the patient unable to exercise; also interrogates valve function and contractile reserve. Best read as a reassurance test given its high NPV.
  • Stress SPECT myocardial perfusion imaging: moderate-to-large reversible defects carry moderate sensitivity for events; absence of reversible defects indicates lower risk; fixed defects do not add perioperative risk.
  • Absolute contraindications to any stress test: ACS, decompensated HF, severe/symptomatic aortic stenosis, uncontrolled arrhythmia, severe hypertension (≥200/110), aortic dissection, peri/myocarditis, pulmonary embolism, severe pulmonary hypertension.

8.  Timing after a recent coronary event or intervention

Where a patient has had a recent coronary event or intervention (the amber branch of the pathway), defer elective arthroplasty as below and re-stratify before listing.

9.  Functional capacity and biomarkers — what to use instead of routine imaging

  • DASI questionnaire (Appendix A) as the structured functional-capacity instrument — it removes the “is it the heart or the hip?” guesswork better than eyeballing. DASI >34 ≈ ≥4 METs: proceed.
  • NT-proBNP (± hs-troponin) in the at-risk patient (age ≥65, or ≥45 with cardiovascular disease, or RCRI ≥1). NT-proBNP <300 ng/L with normal troponin is reassuring; elevation directs stress imaging and postoperative surveillance.
  • CPET reserved for the genuinely ambiguous, comorbid or complex-revision case via the High-Risk Anaesthesia Clinic — the objective gold standard, but resource-heavy and not for the routine pathway.
  • Optimisation of anaemia, blood pressure, glycaemia and smoking delivers more perioperative benefit than any imaging test.

10.  Monitoring, compliance and audit

Compliance with this guideline is monitored by the department clinical governance lead and reported to the Department and Medical Advisory Committee. Baseline audit at implementation, then at 6 and 12 months and annually thereafter.

  • Audit metrics:
    • Proportion of elective arthroplasty patients receiving a resting echo, and the proportion of those that changed management.
    • Proportion of stress-imaging requests satisfying all four gates.
    • List cancellations and total bed-days attributable to awaiting cardiac investigation.
    • DASI documentation rate; NT-proBNP use in the at-risk cohort.
  • Target: elimination of non-indicated resting echocardiography and stress imaging, with no increase in perioperative MACE or unplanned critical-care admission.
  • Exceptions to the pathway are legitimate but must be documented with the clinical reasoning in the patient record.

Appendix A.  Duke Activity Status Index (DASI)

Score = sum of the MET weights for every activity the patient can perform without symptoms (maximum 58.2). Estimated peak VO₂ (ml/kg/min) ≈ 0.43 × DASI + 9.6; estimated METs ≈ VO₂peak / 3.5.

Interpretation: DASI >34 ≈ ≥4 METs (good capacity — proceed). DASI <25 ≈ <4 METs (poor capacity — enter the testing limb of the pathway). DASI 25–34 is indeterminate — use NT-proBNP to refine.

2. Walk indoors, e.g. around your house1.75

7. Do moderate work (vacuuming, sweeping, carrying groceries)3.50
8. Do heavy work (scrubbing floors, moving heavy furniture)8.00
9. Do garden work (raking, weeding, pushing a mower)4.50
10. Have sexual relations5.25
